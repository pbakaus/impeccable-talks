IV · Lessons from building itWhat worked

What worked

01
A shared vocabulary.
The same word means the same move to the human and the model. Direction stops getting lost in translation between deciding and building.
02
Subtract, then add.
Strip the model's defaults first, the slop it reaches for, then hand over the words to steer. Order matters. Vocabulary lands only once the bias is gone.
03
Steer in the running product.
Adjust live, in the browser, on real code. Drag the result instead of re-prompting it. Small visual decisions stop costing a round-trip.
What worked
IV · Lessons from building itWhat didn't

What didn't

01
You can't teach the model taste.
We tried to make it a better designer directly. It doesn't move. Taste is personal and always shifting. You can remove the tells, you can't install the judgment.
02
Some adjectives are too personal.
"Warmer" means one thing to you, another to me. The ambiguous ones need the brief in context, a reference, a point of view. Without it they regress to the average.
03
An adjective with no brief behind it is just a nicer prompt.
The vocabulary only works on top of intent. Hand it to someone with nothing to say and you get output without intent all over again.
What didn't
V · The bigger ideaPast the chat box

This isn't really about design

Find the level the expert
actually thinks in.

Any AI interface where creative intent matters more than parameter control has the same problem. The slider is too low. The chat box is too high. The work is to find the abstraction the expert reaches for, and build the controls there.

For design, that level is the adjective. Find yours.
